Research Design

Hypothesis & analytical approach

In February 2026, Formula 1 brought its Showrun to the streets of San Francisco's Marina neighborhood — a high-speed demonstration event through a dense residential corridor. Having recently studied Difference-in-Differences regression as part of my graduate coursework at USF, I was drawn to the question of whether the method could be applied to something observable and local: did the event cause a measurable increase in 311 service complaints, or was any apparent spike simply noise? The city's publicly available 311 dataset offered a precise, real-time operational signal to test this.

The central question is not whether complaints went up — they almost certainly did. The question is whether the increase can be causally attributed to the event, rather than reflecting broader citywide trends or statistical variation. That distinction — between correlation and causation — is what makes the analysis meaningful and what transforms an observation into something operationally useful.

Null Hypothesis (H₀)
"The F1 Showrun had no statistically significant effect on 311 complaint volume in the Marina neighborhood, after controlling for citywide complaint trends."
If H₀ holds, any increase in Marina complaints during the event period would be no greater than what the citywide baseline would predict — and the event cannot be identified as the causal driver.
Verdict H₀ rejected — p < 0.05 · DiD estimate: +12.31 additional complaints / day

To test this, the analysis applies Difference-in-Differences (DiD) — a quasi-experimental method from econometrics that compares the change in a treatment group (Marina) against the change in a control group (all other SF neighborhoods) across time. The interaction term in the OLS regression isolates the causal effect: how much more did Marina complaints change during the event, relative to the simultaneous change in the rest of the city?

Methodology

From raw data to causal inference

01
Data Loading & Cleaning
Loaded the 311 CSV, parsed date-time strings with lubridate, standardised neighborhood names with janitor's clean_names(), and filtered to the Jan–Mar 2026 window.
02
Treatment & Control Construction
Treatment: Marina daily complaint counts. Control: mean daily complaints across all other SF neighborhoods — computed per day. This produces a city-representative baseline that accounts for citywide variation without being dominated by any single outlier neighborhood.
control_data <- daily_complaints |>
  filter(neighborhood != "Marina") |>
  group_by(date) |>
  summarise(complaints_count = mean(complaints_count))
03
Period Classification & Binary Flags
Three periods: pre-event (before Feb 20), event period (Feb 20–22), post-event (after Feb 22). Binary flags created for the Marina treatment group and event window — enabling the interaction term that gives the DiD estimate.
marina_flag <- ifelse(neighborhood == "Marina", 1, 0)
event_flag <- ifelse(period == "event_period", 1, 0)
04
DiD OLS Regression
OLS with a marina × event interaction term. The interaction coefficient is the DiD estimate — the additional complaints per day in the Marina attributable to the event, after removing the citywide baseline trend captured by the event_flag coefficient alone.
lm(complaints_count ~ marina_flag * event_flag,
   data = analysis_data)

Key Findings

What the regression confirms

PRIMARY FINDING — DiD ESTIMATE · STATISTICALLY SIGNIFICANT
The F1 Showrun caused a neighbourhood-specific, causally identified surge in complaints
+12.31 complaints / day
The OLS interaction term (marina_flag × event_flag) returns +12.31, significant at p < 0.05. This is not a before/after observation — it is a causal estimate that controls for the simultaneous citywide baseline through the DiD design. The null hypothesis is rejected. The event is identified as the driver of the surge.
FINDING 02 — CONTROL GROUP STABILITY
Zero citywide effect — the spike belongs to the Marina alone
~0 citywide Δ
The event_flag coefficient (non-interacted) is near zero and statistically insignificant — confirming that the rest of San Francisco saw no unusual complaint activity during Feb 20–22. Weather, reporting behaviour shifts, and other city-level confounders are ruled out.
FINDING 03 — PARALLEL TRENDS VALIDATED
Causal identification is credible — the key assumption holds
Confirmed H₀ rejected
The time series shows Marina and the control group tracking closely in the pre-event period. This validates the parallel trends assumption — the core requirement for DiD to produce a credible causal estimate. Post-event, Marina returns to its normal baseline, confirming the effect was temporary and event-driven.

Strategic Recommendations

From findings to operational action

The value of a causal, quantified finding is that it is actionable. A +12.31 complaints per day figure is not an observation — it is a planning input. It can directly inform enforcement pre-deployment, staffing models, and event impact scoring for future large-scale events in residential neighborhoods.

STRATEGIC RECOMMENDATION 01 — HIGHEST IMPACT
Integrate DiD-based complaint analysis into the City's standard event impact assessment process
The methodology applied here is fully replicable across any neighborhood-event combination in the 311 dataset. SF's Office of Special Events could adopt DiD as a post-event evaluation tool — running it systematically after Fleet Week, Outside Lands, Bay to Breakers, and other major events. Over two to three cycles, this builds a complaint-impact library with statistically validated benchmarks by event type, neighborhood density, and duration. Those benchmarks can then inform event approval criteria, permit conditions, and public communication plans before events are approved — not after.
Strategic impact: City-wide policy infrastructure
RECOMMENDATION 02 — IMMEDIATE OPERATIONAL
Pre-deploy parking enforcement within a defined radius of the event route before Showrun-scale events begin
46 of the top complaints were blocked driveway reports — 22 requesting citation plus towing. This is not a reactive problem; it is a preventable one. Temporary no-parking signage and enforcement presence deployed 24 hours before event staging begins, within a two-block radius of the route, would materially reduce the +12 complaint/day surge at low marginal cost relative to the enforcement resources already mobilised for crowd management.
Operational impact: Resident experience, complaint reduction
RECOMMENDATION 03 — PLANNING BASELINE
Use +12 complaints/day as the validated planning benchmark for future comparable events in dense residential neighborhoods
A statistically validated complaint-surge figure now exists for an F1-scale street event in a dense SF residential neighborhood. Future event planners can use this as the baseline for 311 volume modeling — sizing 311 call-centre capacity, estimating enforcement resource needs, and setting resident communication expectations before the event takes place, rather than responding reactively once complaints begin filing.
Planning impact: Resource allocation, event approval modeling
Reflection

Why the method matters

The most consequential decision in this project was choosing DiD over a simple before/after comparison. A naive analysis would show that Marina complaints went up — but that finding alone cannot distinguish between the event as a cause and a dozen alternative explanations. DiD rules those alternatives out by using the control group to absorb citywide variation simultaneously.

The parallel trends validation step is not a formality — it is what makes the causal claim defensible. Without it, the +12.31 figure would be a correlation. With it, it is an estimate that a policy analyst or operations planner can take seriously and act on.

"The question is never whether complaints went up — of course they did. The question is whether you can prove it was the event and not something else, and quantify it precisely enough to be useful. That is what the method gives you."
