The Question

Did the F1 Showrun actually cause more complaints?

In February 2026, Formula 1 brought its Showrun event to San Francisco — a high-speed demonstration run through the streets of the Marina neighborhood. For residents, it meant road closures, crowds, and disruption. But how much disruption, exactly? And was any spike in complaints genuinely caused by the event, or just normal variation?

This project uses San Francisco's publicly available 311 service request data to answer that question rigorously — isolating the causal effect of the event from background noise using a Difference-in-Differences regression model.

Research Question
"Did the F1 Showrun cause a statistically significant increase in 311 complaints in the Marina neighborhood, controlling for citywide trends?"
The Data

SF 311 Cases — real open data

The analysis uses San Francisco's live 311 service request dataset, published and maintained by the City on DataSF. Each row represents a single complaint — with a timestamp, neighborhood, complaint type, and status. The dataset is filtered to Supervisor District 2 (which includes the Marina) for the period January 1 to March 1, 2026.

The control group is constructed as the average daily complaints across all other San Francisco neighborhoods — not just one comparison area. This approach captures the citywide baseline more robustly than a single-neighborhood comparison.

Methodology

Why Difference-in-Differences?

A simple before/after comparison of Marina complaints would tell us that complaints went up during the event — but it wouldn't tell us whether that increase was caused by the event, or whether it would have happened anyway as part of a citywide trend. Difference-in-Differences (DiD) solves this problem by comparing the change in Marina against the change in the rest of the city simultaneously.

The key assumption is the parallel trends assumption — that in the absence of the event, Marina and other neighborhoods would have followed similar complaint trajectories. The time series visualization confirms this holds in the pre-event period.

Three periods defined: pre-event (before Feb 20), event period (Feb 20–22), and post-event (after Feb 22). Binary indicator flags created for DiD regression.

OLS regression with an interaction term between the marina flag and event flag. The coefficient on the interaction term gives the DiD estimate — the causal effect of the event on Marina complaints, net of citywide trends.
lm(complaints_count ~ marina_flag * event_flag, data = analysis_data)
Key Findings

What the data shows

FINDING 01 — PRIMARY RESULT
The F1 Showrun caused a statistically significant surge in Marina complaints
+12.31 complaints/day
The DiD coefficient on the interaction term (marina_flag × event_flag) is +12.31 and statistically significant. This is the isolated causal effect of the event on the Marina — net of any citywide trends. The rest of San Francisco remained stable throughout the event period.
FINDING 02 — BASELINE
Marina has a structurally higher complaint baseline
Higher baseline
Even before the event, Marina complaint volumes ran higher than the city average. This is captured by the marina_flag coefficient and confirms the parallel trends assumption holds — the gap is stable pre-event, then widens sharply during the Showrun.
FINDING 03 — ISOLATION
The spike is neighbourhood-specific, not citywide
Isolated effect
Other SF neighborhoods showed no meaningful change in complaint volume during the event window. This rules out external confounders — weather, city-wide policy changes, or reporting behaviour shifts — as alternative explanations for the Marina spike.
FINDING 04 — COMPLAINT TYPE
Blocked driveways dominated event-period complaints
#1 complaint
Blocked driveways were the most common complaint type in Marina during the event window. Of these, 24 requested a citation and 22 requested citation plus towing — indicating complete driveway obstruction in a significant share of cases.

Operational Implications

What this means for city planning

The findings are directly actionable for city operations teams responsible for managing large-scale events. A quantified, statistically validated surge figure — +12.31 complaints per day — can inform resource pre-deployment decisions before future events of similar scale.

🚗
Pre-deploy parking enforcement in the Marina for similar events
Blocked driveways accounted for the two largest complaint categories. Proactive parking enforcement — rather than reactive citation requests — would reduce complaint volume and improve resident experience during future events.
📊
Use the +12 complaint/day figure as a planning baseline
Future event impact assessments for the Marina can use 12 additional complaints per day as a statistically validated benchmark. This enables more accurate staffing and service capacity planning before events are approved.
🏙️
Expand the DiD framework to other event types and neighborhoods
The methodology is replicable across any neighborhood-event combination in the 311 dataset. Applying it to other large SF events — Outside Lands, Fleet Week, Bay to Breakers — would build a comprehensive operational impact library for city planners.
Reflection

What this project demonstrates

The most interesting methodological choice here was using Difference-in-Differences rather than a simple before/after comparison. A naive analysis would show that Marina complaints went up during the event — but without the control group, that finding could just as easily reflect a citywide trend, a data anomaly, or seasonal variation. DiD rules those out.

The parallel trends assumption — that Marina and other neighborhoods would have moved similarly in the absence of the event — is visually confirmed in the pre-event time series. That validation step is what makes the causal claim credible rather than just correlational.

"The question was never whether complaints went up. Of course they did — it was a Formula 1 event on a residential street. The question was whether we could prove it statistically and quantify it precisely enough to be useful for planning. That's what DiD gives you."
